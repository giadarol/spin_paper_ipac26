\begin{abstract}
Precise modelling of spin dynamics is essential for the energy-calibration programme of the FCC-ee collider, where resonant depolarization measurements require accurate predictions of polarization buildup and of the spin-tune response to machine errors. We present spin modeling capabilities recently implemented in the Xsuite simulation framework, enabling high-performance six-dimensional tracking of particle trajectories together with their spin motion. The module includes the integration of the Thomas–BMT equation and a linearized computation of the invariant spin field (ISF), obtained from the one-turn linear map. Radiative spin effects are incorporated through the Sokolov–Ternov mechanism and its generalizations, allowing the evaluation of equilibrium polarization and of its buildup time.
The new tool has been benchmarked against the BMAD code for representative lattices, covering both spin-closed-orbit properties and long-term polarization evolution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Accurate spin-dynamics modeling is a central requirement for the FCC-ee~\cite{fcc_ee_cdr} energy-calibration programme, where resonant depolarization will be used to infer the beam energy with high precision. In this context, tracking tools need to provide not only the orbital motion but also the spin motion, the spin closed orbit, and reliable estimates of polarization buildup and depolarization. Xsuite~\cite{xsuite_reference_paper,xsuite_docs} has been adopted for several FCC design and simulation studies; we therefore decided to introduce spin capabilities to allow its application to spin-related studies.

This paper summarizes recent spin-physics developments in Xsuite. The implementation covers single-particle spin transport based on the Thomas--BMT equation, a first-order computation of the invariant spin field from the one-turn map, and the evaluation of equilibrium polarization and polarization time including radiative effects. The new implementation has been benchmarked against BMAD~\cite{Sagan:BmadManual}.

\section{Spin Tracking Model}

The spin motion is described through the Thomas--BMT equation~\cite{hoffstaetter2006high}. For a particle traveling in a magnetic field $\bm{B}$, the spin-precession angular velocity can be written as
\begin{equation}
\bm{\Omega}_{\mathrm{BMT}} = -\frac{1}{B\rho_{\mathrm{part}}}
\left[(1 + a\gamma)\bm{B}_{\perp} + (1 + a)\bm{B}_{\parallel}\right],
\end{equation}
where $B\rho_{\mathrm{part}}$ is the particle magnetic rigidity, $\gamma$ is the Lorentz factor, $a$ is the anomalous magnetic moment, and $\bm{B}_{\parallel}$ and $\bm{B}_{\perp}$ are the magnetic-field components parallel and transverse to the particle velocity. Over an element of length $\ell$, the spin-precession angle is $\phi = |\bm{\Omega}|\ell$.

In the Xsuite implementation, the spin vector is advanced through the corresponding three-dimensional rotation at each integration step. Using the unit vector $\bm{\omega} = \bm{\Omega}/|\bm{\Omega}|$, the spin transport is represented in terms of the following coefficients
\begin{align}
t_0 &= \cos\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right), &
t_x &= \omega_x\sin\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right), \\
t_y &= \omega_y\sin\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right), &
t_s &= \omega_z\sin\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right),
\end{align}
from which the standard rotation matrix is constructed,
\begin{equation}
\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{$
\mathbf{M} =
\begin{pmatrix}
(t_0^2 + t_x^2) - (t_s^2 + t_y^2) & 2(t_x t_y - t_0 t_s) & 2(t_x t_s + t_0 t_y) \\
2(t_x t_y + t_0 t_s) & (t_0^2 + t_y^2) - (t_x^2 + t_s^2) & 2(t_s t_y - t_0 t_x) \\
2(t_x t_s - t_0 t_y) & 2(t_s t_y + t_0 t_x) & (t_0^2 + t_s^2) - (t_x^2 + t_y^2)
\end{pmatrix}$},
\end{equation}
and applied to the spin coordinates. Such implementation is used in the tracking module and is also exploited by the Twiss module to compute closed-orbit spin components and linear polarization estimates.

\section{Spin and Polarization Calculations in Twiss Module}

The Xsuite Twiss module can optionally analyze spin motion together with the orbital optics. It first computes the closed orbit and the corresponding closed-spin solution $\bm{n}_0$ through a numerical fixed-point search. Because spin does not affect the orbital motion, this calculation is naturally split into a six-dimensional closed-orbit search followed by a three-dimensional fixed-point search for the spin. 

The spin motion is then analyzed as follows.
We introduce the combined coordinate vector
\begin{equation}
\bm{z} = (x, p_x, y, p_y, \zeta, \delta, s_x, s_y, s_z)^T,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\bm{z}_{\mathrm{orb}} = (x, p_x, y, p_y, \zeta, \delta)^T,
\end{equation}
are the orbital coordinates and
\begin{equation}
\bm{z}_{\mathrm{spin}} = (s_x, s_y, s_z)^T,
\end{equation}
are the three spin components.

The one-turn map linearized around the closed orbit can be written as:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{R} =
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{orb}} & 0 \\
\mathbf{D} & \mathbf{A}
\end{pmatrix}.
\label{eq:linear_map}
\end{equation}
Here $\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{orb}}$ is the $6\times6$ orbital map, $\mathbf{A}$ is the $3\times3$ spin block, and $\mathbf{D}$ describes the first-order coupling from orbital coordinates to spin. Let $\bm{e}_i$ and $\lambda_i$ denote the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of $\mathbf{R}$, such that $\mathbf{R}\bm{e}_i = \lambda_i\bm{e}_i$. 
Using the block structure of $\mathbf{R}$, we can write:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{orb}}\,\bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{orb}} &= \lambda_i\bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{orb}},
\label{eq:orbital_eigenmode}\\
\mathbf{D}\,\bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{orb}} + \mathbf{A}\,\bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{spin}} &= \lambda_i\bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{spin}}.
\label{eq:spin_block_relation}
\end{align}
This shows that the orbital part of the 9D eigenvector $\bm{e}_i$ is an eigenvector of the orbital map $\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{orb}}$, while the spin part is related to the orbital part through
\begin{equation}
\bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{spin}} = (\lambda_i\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{A})^{-1}
\mathbf{D}\,\bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{orb}}.
\label{eq:spin_from_orbital_mode}
\end{equation}
Once the eigenvectors are computed at a given location, they can be propagated to any other location along the ring by tracking particles having the eigenvectors as initial conditions (suitably scaled to remain in the linear regime).

From the eigenvectors we can obtain to first order the Invariant Spin Field (ISF) function~\cite{Chao:1980fz,hoffstaetter2006high}, which is necessary for the evaluation of radiative depolarization effects.
Following Ref.~\cite{Chao:1980fz}, the ISF function expanded to first order around the closed orbit can be written as
\begin{equation}
\bm{n}(\bm{z}_{\mathrm{orb}}) = \bm{n}_0 + \mathbf{N}
\left(\bm{z}_{\mathrm{orb}} - \bm{z}_{\mathrm{orb}}^{\mathrm{CO}}\right).
\label{eq:isf_linear_expansion}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{N}$ is a $3\times6$ matrix to be found.
Dropping the constant terms in Eq.~(\ref{eq:isf_linear_expansion}) for the linear derivation, one may write, for a particle having spin aligned with the ISF,
\begin{equation}
\bm{z}_{1,\mathrm{spin}} = \mathbf{N}\bm{z}_{1,\mathrm{orb}}.
\label{eq:isf_at_z1}
\end{equation}
After one turn we get (Eq.~(\ref{eq:linear_map})):
\begin{align}
\bm{z}_{2,\mathrm{orb}} &= \mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{orb}}\bm{z}_{1,\mathrm{orb}},
\label{eq:z2_orbital}\\
\bm{z}_{2,\mathrm{spin}} &= \mathbf{D}\bm{z}_{1,\mathrm{orb}} + \mathbf{A}\bm{z}_{1,\mathrm{spin}}.
\label{eq:z2_spin}
\end{align}
By definition of the ISF, the spin after one turn must still be aligned with the ISF at the new orbital coordinates, which gives:
\begin{equation}
\bm{z}_{2,\mathrm{spin}} = \mathbf{N}\bm{z}_{2,\mathrm{orb}}.
\label{eq:isf_at_z2}
\end{equation}
Combining Eqs.~(\ref{eq:isf_at_z1}), (\ref{eq:z2_orbital}), (\ref{eq:z2_spin}), and (\ref{eq:isf_at_z2}) yields the following relation for the ISF matrix $\mathbf{N}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{N}\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{orb}}\bm{z}_{1,\mathrm{orb}} = \mathbf{D}\bm{z}_{1,\mathrm{orb}} + \mathbf{A}\mathbf{N}\bm{z}_{1,\mathrm{orb}}.
\label{eq:isf_invariance_map}
\end{equation}
Choosing $\bm{z}_{1,\mathrm{orb}} = \bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{orb}}$ and then using Eq.~(\ref{eq:orbital_eigenmode}) in Eq.~(\ref{eq:isf_invariance_map}) gives
\begin{equation}
(\lambda_i\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{A})\mathbf{N}\bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{orb}} = \mathbf{D}\bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{orb}}.
\label{eq:isf_mode_relation}
\end{equation}
Comparing Eq.~(\ref{eq:isf_mode_relation}) with Eq.~(\ref{eq:spin_from_orbital_mode}), one obtains
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{N}\bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{orb}} = \bm{e}_{i,\mathrm{spin}}.
\label{eq:isf_action_on_modes}
\end{equation}
Applying Eq.~(\ref{eq:isf_action_on_modes}) to the six orbital eigenvectors, we obtain the ISF matrix $\mathbf{N}$ as
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{N} = \mathbf{E}^{\mathrm{spin}}\left(\mathbf{E}^{\mathrm{orb}}\right)^{-1},
\label{eq:isf_matrix_from_eigenvectors}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{E}^{\mathrm{orb}}$ collects the six orbital eigenvectors and $\mathbf{E}^{\mathrm{spin}}$ the corresponding spin components. In particular, the last column of $\mathbf{N}$ provides $d\bm{n}/d\delta$, which is needed in the evaluation of radiative depolarization.

The computation of equilibrium polarization and polarization time is based on the standard Sokolov--Ternov/Derbenev--Kondratenko formalism~\cite{Derbenev:1973ia,Boge:1994we,Mane:1986qy,Barber:1999xm}. The beam polarization evolves as
\begin{equation}
P(t) = P(0)e^{-t/\tau_{\mathrm{tot}}} + P_{\mathrm{eq}}
\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau_{\mathrm{tot}}}\right),
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{eq}} = \frac{8}{5\sqrt{3}}\,\frac{\alpha_-}{\alpha_+},
\qquad
\tau_{\mathrm{tot}}^{-1} = \frac{5\sqrt{3}}{8}\frac{r_e\hbar\gamma^5}{m_e}\alpha_+.
\end{equation}
In the linearized treatment, the relevant ring integrals are approximated by
\begin{align}
\alpha_+ &\simeq \frac{1}{C}\oint \frac{ds}{|\rho(s)|^3}
\left[1 - \frac{2}{9}(\bm{n}_0 \cdot \hat{\bm{i}}_v)^2
+ \frac{11}{18}\left|\frac{\partial \bm{n}}{\partial \delta}\right|^2\right]_s, \\
\alpha_- &\simeq \frac{1}{C}\oint \frac{ds}{|\rho(s)|^3}
\left[\hat{\bm{i}}_B \cdot
\left(\bm{n}_0 - \frac{\partial \bm{n}}{\partial \gamma}\right)\right]_s.
\end{align}
where $\hat{\bm{i}}_B$ and $\hat{\bm{i}}_v$ are unit vectors along the magnetic field and the particle velocity, respectively, $\rho(s)$ is the local bending radius, and $C$ is the ring circumference.
The total buildup rate can be decomposed into a polarization term and a depolarization contribution:
\begin{equation}
\tau_{\mathrm{tot}}^{-1} = \tau_{\mathrm{pol}}^{-1} + \tau_{\mathrm{depol}}^{-1},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{align}
\tau_{\mathrm{pol}}^{-1} &= \frac{5\sqrt{3}}{8}\frac{r_e\hbar\gamma^5}{m_e}\frac{1}{C}
\oint \frac{ds}{|\rho(s)|^3}
\left[1 - \frac{2}{9}(\bm{n}_0 \cdot \hat{\bm{i}}_v)^2\right]_s, \\
\tau_{\mathrm{depol}}^{-1} &= \frac{5\sqrt{3}}{8}\frac{r_e\hbar\gamma^5}{m_e}\frac{1}{C}
\oint \frac{ds}{|\rho(s)|^3}
\left[\frac{11}{18}\left|\frac{\partial \bm{n}}{\partial \delta}\right|^2\right]_s.
\end{align}

To account for nonlinear effects, Xsuite also supports a Monte Carlo approach in which the depolarization time is estimated from tracking with synchrotron-radiation quantum excitation~\cite{DUAN201581}. Defining the asymptotic polarization without depolarization as
\begin{equation}
P_{\infty} = \frac{8}{5\sqrt{3}}
\frac{\oint \frac{ds}{|\rho(s)|^3}
\left[\hat{\bm{i}}_B \cdot \bm{n}_0\right]_s}
{\oint \frac{ds}{|\rho(s)|^3}
\left[1 - \frac{2}{9}(\bm{n}_0 \cdot \hat{\bm{i}}_v)^2\right]_s},
\end{equation}
the nonlinear estimate can be written as
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{eq}} = P_{\infty}\left(1 + \frac{\tau_{\mathrm{pol}}}{\tau_{\mathrm{depol}}}\right)^{-1},
\end{equation}
with $\tau_{\mathrm{tot}}^{-1} = \tau_{\mathrm{pol}}^{-1} + \tau_{\mathrm{depol}}^{-1}$. In this way, a tracking-based estimate of $\tau_{\mathrm{depol}}$ can be combined with the linear formalism to obtain practical nonlinear estimates of equilibrium polarization and buildup time.

\section{Benchmark Strategy}

The new implementation has been checked against BMAD for representative lepton-ring lattices, with particular attention to the LEP1 configuration including orbit bumps used to compensate the precession induced by the experimental solenoids. The comparison addresses both deterministic quantities, such as the spin closed orbit and the first-order invariant spin field, and radiative observables, such as equilibrium polarization and polarization buildup time.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth,trim=4mm 0 10mm 0,clip]{orbit_and_spin.png}
\caption{Comparison between Xsuite and BMAD for the LEP1 benchmark with compensation bumps. The figure shows the closed vertical orbit and the corresponding spin components along the ring.}
\label{fig:orbit_spin_benchmark}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:orbit_spin_benchmark} shows that the two codes provide nearly identical closed-orbit and spin patterns around the machine. This validates both the closed-spin computation and the linearized spin transport used in Xsuite.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{polarization.png}
\caption{Equilibrium polarization as a function of bump strength for the LEP1 benchmark. Xsuite and BMAD are in close agreement over the full scan.}
\label{fig:polarization_benchmark}
\end{figure}

The global polarization estimate is also well reproduced, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:polarization_benchmark}. In addition, consistent results were observed against historical SLIM simulations for the same LEP1 setup, providing an independent cross-check of the implementation.

\section{Conclusion}

Xsuite now includes a consistent set of spin-dynamics tools covering Thomas--BMT tracking, first-order invariant-spin-field computation, and the evaluation of equilibrium polarization and polarization time. These capabilities are available within the same tracking and Twiss workflow used for broader Xsuite studies, which is important for large-scale FCC-ee applications. Benchmark comparisons for LEP1 show good agreement with BMAD and are consistent with historical SLIM simulations, supporting the use of the new module for precision spin studies relevant to energy calibration.
